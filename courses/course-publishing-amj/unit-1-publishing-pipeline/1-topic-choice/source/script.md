## Segment 03
Voiceover:
Criterion one: significance. The editors frame this through the lens of grand challenges. The term comes from mathematician David Hilbert, whose list of unsolved problems has driven innovation for over a century. In management research, a grand challenge means tackling large, unresolved problems in a bold and unconventional way that leaps beyond existing explanations. Ferlie and colleagues asked why evidence-based innovations fail to spread in healthcare, confronting a linear diffusion model and revealing that professionalization can create nonspread. Your topic does not need to eradicate global poverty, but it must confront a meaningful puzzle in its literature.

## Segment 04
Voiceover:
Criterion two: novelty. Think of scientific work as a conversation among scholars. A novel topic does not merely add to the existing momentum; it causes heads to turn as the conversation darts in an entirely new direction. Sometimes novelty comes from introducing new vocabulary, new constructs. Other times it comes from building a bridge between two literatures that rarely speak to each other. The editors warn of three traps. The familiarity trap: picking a topic that feels like a marginal extension. The maturity trap: choosing something so well-studied that the contribution feels redundant. And the nearness trap: working in a space so adjacent to existing work that it overlaps too much. Agarwal and colleagues avoided all three traps by studying spin-outs: entrepreneurial ventures started by former employees competing against their own former firms.

## Segment 05
Voiceover:
Criterion three: curiosity. A novel topic draws a reader in, but it takes something more to catch and hold their attention. Davis proposed that topics are interesting when their propositions counter a reader's taken-for-granted assumptions. A study showing something seemingly good to be bad arouses curiosity because it challenges initial expectations. Alvesson and Kärreman take this further with the metaphor of mystery: breakdowns in data or theory that cannot be explained by existing models. When more reading and more questions fail to resolve the puzzle, a mystery is at hand. Think of Agatha Christie's And Then There Were None. The story is a page-turner for one simple reason: the reader does not know the ending. If a reviewer can guess your results from your title alone, you have a curiosity problem.

Prompt: Top-center small label "Criterion 3" and bold headline "Curiosity — The Mystery Test" with yellow highlight on "Mystery Test." Below, a two-column comparison layout separated by a thin vertical line. ## Segment 06
Voiceover:
Criterion four: scope. Even the best topic ideas can be undermined if the resulting study is too small. Scope is the degree to which the landscape involved in a topic is adequately sampled, in terms of relevant constructs, mechanisms, and perspectives. The editors are blunt: AMJ does not publish research notes. Anecdotally, they suspect many submissions slice their data too thin, trying to get multiple good papers out of a dataset rather than one great one. The best topics set out to fully and comprehensively sample the landscape in a given domain. Seibert, Kraimer, and Liden's study of social capital and career success is their example: instead of testing one theoretical perspective, they tested all three, operationalizing mediators for each.

## Segment 07
Voiceover:
Criterion five: actionability. A topic should offer insights for managerial or organizational practice. McGahan outlines five pathways: offering counterintuitive insights, highlighting the effect of new and important practices, showing inconsistencies in existing practices, suggesting a specific theory to explain an interesting situation, and identifying an iconic phenomenon that opens new areas of inquiry. Vermeulen complements this by noting that research has relevance when it generates insights practitioners find useful for understanding their own organizational realities, especially about variables within the control of managers. Consider emerging innovations with low capital intensity and restricted R&D. A twenty-dollar artificial knee and low-cost medical equipment remain white spaces in both the competitive and academic sense. That inherent actionability makes the topic compelling.

## Segment 08
Voiceover:
Let us see all five criteria together. An effective topic allows researchers to tackle a grand challenge in a literature, pursue a novel direction that arouses and maintains curiosity, build a study with ambitious scope, and uncover actionable insights. The editors contrast these against their weaker counterparts: modest, incremental, intuitive, narrow, or irrelevant. Given that topic choice is one of the least revisable aspects of any submission, they urge future submitters to ask frank and critical colleagues for feedback on their topic choices, especially if those colleagues are familiar with AMJ.

## Segment 09
Voiceover:
Here is your takeaway checklist. Before committing to a topic, run it through these five filters. Does it address a grand challenge in the literature? Would it change the ongoing conversation rather than just add to it? Can a reviewer guess the ending from the title? Is the scope ambitious enough for a forty-page AMJ article? And does it offer something managers can act on? If the answer to any of these is no, reconsider before investing years of your career. And remember: get frank feedback from colleagues who know the journal. Topic choice is the one decision you cannot undo in a revision. In the next lesson, we turn to research design: how to match your design to your question so that your study can actually deliver on the promise your topic makes.
